How to choose the right cocktail glasses

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Start with three essentials: a rocks glass, a highball glass, and a coupe or martini glass. Focus on durability over decoration, tempered glass or lead-free crystal holds up to everyday use and the dishwasher. Match the glass shape to your favorite drinks, not to trends, and buy a set of four to six so you can host without stress.

What types of cocktail glasses do I actually need?

You don’t need a dozen different shapes to make good drinks at home. Most cocktail enthusiasts agree that three glasses cover the vast majority of recipes: a rocks glass (also called an Old Fashioned or lowball glass) for spirit-forward drinks served on ice, a highball glass for tall mixed drinks like a Tom Collins or Mojito, and a stemmed glass, either a coupe or a classic V-shaped martini glass, for stirred or shaken cocktails served up. A coupe is often more practical than a martini glass because its rounded bowl is less prone to spills and works equally well for a Daiquiri or a Martini. If you only buy one stemmed option, many home bartenders recommend the coupe. A versatile white wine glass can also pull double duty for certain cocktails, but the three core types above will have you covered for 90% of what you might mix.

Are stemmed or stemless glasses better for cocktails?

Stemmed glasses keep your hand away from the bowl so the drink stays cold longer, a real advantage for a chilled Martini or a Sazerac. The stem also lets you swirl the drink without warming it, and it gives a classic, elegant look that many entertaining hosts prefer. Stemless glasses are sturdier and much less likely to tip over, and they stack easily in a cupboard. They also feel more casual and comfortable to hold. The trade-off is that your hand will warm the liquid faster, so stemless rocks glasses are fine for drinks meant to be sipped with lots of ice, but they’re not ideal for cocktails intended to be served very cold and straight up. Your choice really depends on whether you prioritize chill retention or everyday practicality.

What should I look for in glass quality?

First, check whether the glass is made from tempered soda-lime glass or lead-free crystal. Tempered glass is very durable and can survive the dishwasher and accidental knocks, it’s what most commercial bars use. Lead-free crystal (often labeled as “crystal glass” or “premium crystal”) adds clarity and a thin, elegant rim, but it’s more fragile and often not dishwasher-safe. If you want low-maintenance glasses, pick tempered. Second, feel the rim. A good glass should have a smooth, even lip, run your finger around it before buying. Thin rims make sipping more comfortable; thick, rolled rims feel clumsy. Third, consider weight. Heavier glasses feel substantial in the hand and are less likely to chip, but lightweight crystal gives a delicate drinking experience. Neither is objectively better, match it to your own preference.

How many cocktail glasses should I buy for a home bar?

For most households, a set of four to six glasses of each core type is the sweet spot. Four covers a couple or a small dinner party, while six lets you comfortably host a few friends without scrambling. If you entertain larger groups regularly, a dozen of the most-used shape, usually the rocks glass, is a smart investment. Buying full sets is often cheaper per glass than picking individual pieces, and you get matching stems or rims. But there is also charm in collecting mismatched vintage glasses over time. The key is to ensure every glass in your bar is one you genuinely enjoy drinking from, quantity is less important than quality and comfort.

Is there a difference between a cocktail glass and a martini glass?

Strictly speaking, a “cocktail glass” is a broad category that includes the classic V-shaped martini glass, the coupe, and even the Nick & Nora glass (a small, round-bowled stemmed glass). In everyday speech, people often use “cocktail glass” to mean a stemmed glass with a wide, shallow bowl, but that shape is actually a martini glass. The martini glass has a distinctive angled cone, it’s designed to let the aromatics of a dry Martini or a Straight Up cocktail hit your nose as you sip. The coupe, meanwhile, has a rounded bowl that holds smaller portions and is less prone to spills. For your home bar, the practical difference is minor: choose whatever feels best in your hand. Many drinkers now prefer the coupe because it’s more stable and versatile.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use wine glasses for cocktails?

Yes, absolutely. A white wine glass works well for tall, fizzy cocktails like a French 75 or a Bellini, and a red wine glass can substitute for a rocks glass in a pinch. The wide bowl also aerates spirit-forward drinks nicely. Just be aware that wine glasses are more fragile than dedicated cocktail glasses and may not look as polished on a bar.

What's the best glass for an Old Fashioned?

The classic choice is a rocks glass, a short, heavy-bottomed tumbler with a wide mouth. This shape allows you to muddle sugar or fruit if you like, and it holds a large ice cube or multiple smaller cubes comfortably. A double Old Fashioned glass is even taller and holds a bigger pour, which many home bartenders prefer.

Are cheap cocktail glasses okay to start with?

They can be, especially if you’re on a tight budget. Many inexpensive glasses are still tempered and dishwasher-safe. The main downsides are a thicker rim (which can feel less comfortable) and a tendency to develop scratches over time. If you plan to drink cocktails regularly, investing a moderate amount in lead-free crystal or premium tempered glass will improve the experience noticeably.

How do I care for cocktail glasses?

Hand-wash stemmed glasses with warm water and mild soap to avoid chipping and clouding. Let them air-dry on a soft towel or a drying mat. For rocks and highball glasses, a dishwasher is usually fine if the glass is tempered and placed securely. Always check the manufacturer’s care instructions, many lead crystal pieces explicitly say “hand wash only.” Avoid abrasive scrub pads on any glass.

Should I get etched or plain glasses?

Etched glasses add grip and can prevent your hand from slipping, which is handy if you’re serving outdoors or you tend to sweat. They also hide light scratches better. Plain glasses look cleaner and show off the color of your cocktail. It’s purely a visual and tactile preference, no practical difference in how the drink tastes.

What about novelty or oversized glasses?

Novelty shapes, like skull-shaped or giant fishbowl glasses, are best reserved for special occasions or Instagram photos. They rarely pour a standard drink size and can be uncomfortable to hold. Stick to traditional shapes for daily use. If you want a conversation piece, buy one or two novelty rocks glasses, but don’t make them your everyday go-to.

In shortBuilding a cocktail glass collection doesn’t have to be expensive or complicated. Start with a set of rocks glasses and a couple of stemmed options, coupes are especially buyer-friendly, and add shapes as your home bar grows. The best glass is the one you reach for naturally, so prioritize feel and durability over fashion. Sip well.
